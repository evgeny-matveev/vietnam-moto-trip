# Highlands south, coast home

## At a glance

- **12 days** from Đà Nẵng to Đà Lạt and home along the coast
- **Approximately 1,650–1,800 km** on the loaded scooters
- **One full Đà Lạt hiking day** and **one full Nha Trang day**
- **Road priority:** attractive provincial, coastal and village roads; busy highways only where the realistic alternatives are worse
- **Trip window:** late September to early October 2026

This is one route, not a menu of competing itineraries. It runs south through the highlands while the group is fresh, reaches the coast at Nha Trang, and makes the later days more flexible. The finish is deliberately easy and close to services if the weather becomes unsettled.

The line does not pretend every kilometre is quiet. Day 4 uses QL14 because no sensible parallel route covers the same transfer. Days 9–11 may need short QL1A connectors when a coastal road is closed, flooded or incomplete. Those compromises are named rather than hidden.

## Conditions for late September 2026

**Last reviewed: 16 July 2026.** This is planning guidance, not a forecast for the actual ride.

The [National Center for Hydro-Meteorological Forecasting seasonal outlook](https://nchmf.gov.vn/kttvsite/vi-VN/1/ban-tin-du-bao-canh-bao-khi-hau-thoi-han-mua-tren-pham-vi-toan-quoc-tu-thang-8-2026-01-2027-post53548.html) expects September rainfall near the long-term average across most of the route, with above-normal heat and continued potential for heavy rain. That does not make the highlands dry: afternoon rain, low cloud and wet pass surfaces remain realistic early in the trip.

Nha Trang and the central coast are entering their wetter September–December period. Sea conditions can change the Day 8 plan even when the city itself is usable. Check the [official short-range forecasts and warnings](https://nchmf.gov.vn/kttvsite/) before each riding day and again before any boat booking. There is intentionally no live weather feed in this static guide.

Three decisions matter more than a rigid schedule:

1. Do not enter Vi Ô Lắc or Khánh Lê in heavy rain, lightning or dense fog. Wait somewhere safe with services.
2. Remove side trips before removing the daylight margin. Waterfalls, capes and viewpoints are the first things to skip.
3. Treat local closure information as more reliable than the drawn line on ĐT639 and low coastal lanes.

## Day 1 — Đà Nẵng to Tam Thanh

**Road:** Non Nước → Hội An countryside → Cửa Đại → quiet Quảng Nam coast

**Distance:** 95–115 km · **Riding:** 3–4 hours

Start gently. Non Nước and the Marble Mountains make sense early, before their steps become hot or crowded. Around Hội An, use rice-field roads and park outside the old-town pedestrian core. Continue by Cửa Đại and the open coastal lanes rather than joining QL1A.

Choose one longer stop: Marble Mountains, Hội An, or an unhurried beach pause at An Bàng or Cửa Đại. Walk Tam Thanh’s mural lanes near sunset.

**If weather turns:** Skip the Marble Mountains’ slick steps and take the direct surfaced countryside line to Tam Thanh.

## Day 2 — Tam Thanh to Măng Đen

**Road:** Tam Thanh → Ba Tơ → QL24 → Vi Ô Lắc Pass → Măng Đen

**Distance:** 235–250 km · **Riding:** 6–7 hours

This is the first serious day. Leave early, eat in Ba Tơ and begin the climb with fuel and daylight in reserve. Traffic falls away as QL24 rises toward Vi Ô Lắc. Use only pull-offs that are fully separated from the road.

Pa Sỹ Waterfall, Đăk Ke Lake and Kon Pring village are arrival-day alternatives. Pick one only when the group reaches Măng Đen early enough.

**If weather turns:** If Vi Ô Lắc is hidden by heavy rain or dense cloud, wait in Ba Tơ. Do not begin a late, blind pass crossing.

## Day 3 — Măng Đen to Pleiku

**Road:** Măng Đen → Kon Tum → Biển Hồ lanes → Pleiku

**Distance:** 100–125 km · **Riding:** 3–4 hours

Use the short distance well. In Kon Tum, pair the Wooden Church with the Kon Klor communal house and bridge. Kon Kơ Tu is a living Ba Na community: ask before taking photographs and do not treat homes as exhibits.

Near Pleiku, choose Biển Hồ and tea lanes or the longer Chư Đăng Ya detour. The latter needs a dry, unhurried afternoon.

**If weather turns:** Skip Chư Đăng Ya’s exposed and potentially muddy slopes; ride directly to Pleiku.

## Day 4 — Pleiku to Lắk Lake

**Road:** Pleiku → Chư Sê → Buôn Hồ → Buôn Ma Thuột bypass → Lắk Lake

**Distance:** 225–245 km · **Riding:** 6–7 hours

This is the unavoidable QL14 transfer. Expect buses, trucks, road noise and less room for scenic wandering. Leave early, keep a wide following margin and avoid sitting beside heavy vehicles. Use plantation side roads only when they clearly rejoin without a large distance penalty.

Dray Sáp or Dray Nur is a major substitution, not a quick extra. Taking either waterfall means dropping city and plantation stops. Lắk Lake at the end of the day is the fixed reward.

**If weather turns:** Remove every waterfall and plantation detour. Continue on the direct surfaced line, or stop in Buôn Ma Thuột if thunderstorms destroy visibility.

## Day 5 — Lắk Lake to Đà Lạt

**Road:** QL27 → Đinh Văn area → Nam Ban → ĐT725 → Tà Nung → Đà Lạt

**Distance:** 180–200 km · **Riding:** 5–6 hours

The western approach is slower than a main-road transfer and much more satisfying. It moves through villages, coffee country and the Tà Nung climb. Surfaces and drainage can change after rain, so the day should feel measured rather than fast.

Take one coffee stop. Elephant Waterfall is optional and should be attempted only if access is officially open, the steps are safe and timing remains comfortable.

**If weather turns:** Skip the waterfall and unpaved farm lanes. Stay on the main surfaced Nam Ban–Tà Nung connector.

## Day 6 — Đà Lạt hiking day

Leave the scooters and luggage parked. These are alternative days, not a checklist:

- **Lang Biang:** the substantial choice, roughly 9 km with 650–700 m of ascent.
- **Đa Phú:** a shorter ridge and pine-hill option, best with clear early or late light.
- **Tuyền Lâm:** the gentlest choice, using pine lanes and lakeside paths.

Choose after seeing the morning cloud, rain and group energy. After Lang Biang, keep the rest of the day empty.

**If weather turns:** Leave exposed trails immediately in thunder. Persistent rain turns this into a recovery day around Tuyền Lâm, cafés and the city.

## Day 7 — Đà Lạt to Nha Trang

**Road:** Đà Lạt → Bidoup–Núi Bà / Hòn Giao → Khánh Lê Pass → Khánh Vĩnh → Nha Trang

**Distance:** 135–145 km · **Riding:** 4–5 hours

The forest transition around Hòn Giao and the long descent are the day. Descending puts sustained demand on small-scooter brakes: use engine braking where possible, leave distance, stop to cool the machines and never chase a faster rider. Read the [Khánh Hòa Police pass guidance](https://congan.khanhhoa.gov.vn/vi/ky-nang-can-thiet-khi-lai-xe-tren-duong-deo-khanh-le) and verify the same-day road status because landslide restrictions can change.

Bidoup–Núi Bà is a landscape stop on this transit, not a second trekking day. Keep the first Nha Trang evening free for the beach and food.

**If weather turns:** Do not start Khánh Lê in heavy rain or dense fog. Wait in Đà Lạt. If the bad conditions persist, arrange vehicle transport instead of forcing the pass.

## Day 8 — Full Nha Trang day

There is no intercity riding and no obligation to combine both plans.

### Good sea conditions

Book one established island or snorkelling trip around Nha Trang Bay and Hòn Mun after checking official marine warnings and the operator’s current permits and safety arrangements.

### Poor sea conditions

Build a relaxed city day from Po Nagar, Long Sơn Pagoda, the National Oceanographic Museum, Hòn Chồng and a mud bath. Three well-chosen stops are enough.

The [official Nha Trang guide](https://vietnam.travel/places-to-go/central-vietnam/nha-trang) is the starting point for both plans.

**If weather turns:** Cancel boat activity in warnings, swell or thunderstorms. The city-and-mud-bath plan is the complete alternative, not a consolation prize.

## Day 9 — Nha Trang to Sông Cầu

**Road:** Nha Trang → Đại Lãnh → Phú Yên coast → Tuy Hòa → Sông Cầu

**Distance:** 210–235 km · **Riding:** 5–7 hours

Choose the day’s substantial branch at breakfast:

- **South Phú Yên:** Vũng Rô, Bãi Môn and Mũi Điện.
- **North Phú Yên:** Ô Loan lagoon and Gành Đá Đĩa.

The direct route includes short QL1A sections where there is no reasonable parallel connector. Do not attempt both branches unless the group deliberately accepts a very long day. Finish in Sông Cầu, with the notable QL1D viewpoints saved for the next morning if needed.

**If weather turns:** Drop cape, reef and minor-lane detours. Use the direct surfaced route through Tuy Hòa.

## Day 10 — Sông Cầu to Sa Huỳnh

**Road:** QL1D → Quy Nhơn → Cát Tiến → Đề Gi → Lộ Diêu → Sa Huỳnh

**Distance:** 180–215 km · **Riding:** 5–7 hours

Stop meaningfully in Quy Nhơn rather than circling every landmark. Ghềnh Ráng and Tháp Đôi are the easiest city choices. Bánh Ít, Eo Gió and Ông Núi pull in different directions, so choose one wider detour at most.

This day is provisional. Parts of ĐT639 remained tied to a construction programme extending into 2026. Read the [published project schedule](https://baodautu.vn/binh-dinh-du-an-tuyen-duong-ven-bien-tang-von-dau-tu-hon-230-ty-dong-d205710.html), then ask locally about the exact open sections before leaving Quy Nhơn.

**If weather or construction closes the coast:** Take the shortest open connector to QL1A, accept the busy section and return to the coast only where the road is confirmed open. Reach Sa Huỳnh before dark.

## Day 11 — Sa Huỳnh to Tam Thanh

**Road:** Gò Cỏ → Mỹ Khê → Sơn Mỹ → Bà Làng An → inland Dung Quất bypass → Tam Thanh

**Distance:** 175–200 km · **Riding:** 5–6 hours

Start with Gò Cỏ or a longer Sa Huỳnh morning. Give the Sơn Mỹ Memorial the quiet time appropriate to its history. Mỹ Khê, Bà Làng An and Thiên Ấn Pagoda are choices around that fixed stop.

The route bends inland around Dung Quất to avoid industrial truck corridors. Do not improvise through the port approaches simply because they look shorter on a small map.

**If weather turns:** Remove exposed beaches and capes, avoid flooded low lanes and use the direct inland Quảng Ngãi connector to Tam Thanh.

## Day 12 — Tam Thanh to Đà Nẵng

**Road:** Tam Kỳ countryside → Duy Xuyên → Hội An rice fields → Đà Nẵng

**Distance:** 95–120 km · **Riding:** 3–4 hours

The return uses inland parallel roads so it does not simply repeat Day 1. Stop for lunch around Hội An, then reach Đà Nẵng with time to refuel, return the scooters and deal calmly with wet luggage or small mechanical delays.

**If weather turns:** Rice-field lanes can flood quickly. Use the most direct open surfaced route and protect the arrival time.

## How to use the places on the map

The map groups places into waterfalls, parks and reserves, natural landmarks, viewpoints, culture and spiritual sites, and history and monuments. Select a marker to see why it is worthwhile, the time to allow, approximate detour and any access or seasonal note.

When the whole loop is selected, every route place is visible. Focusing on one day shows only places associated with that day. The markers are a decision aid: each day’s text tells you which stops compete with one another.

Coordinates are approximate planning points. Descriptions use official tourism, park, heritage or government sources linked from each place card. Confirm opening, access and local restrictions before riding.

## Final checks before departure

- Read the [latest official forecast and warnings](https://nchmf.gov.vn/kttvsite/) for the next riding day.
- Ask the previous night’s accommodation about pass visibility, landslides, flooding and ĐT639 construction.
- Agree on a latest departure and a daylight stop rule before Days 2, 4, 7, 9, 10 and 11.
- Treat QL14 trucks and any emergency QL1A connector as a disclosed risk, not a place to make up time.
- If a road feels wrong, stop together. The relaxed plan has enough margin to wait or remove a detour.
